Abstract

Restoring high-fidelity audio from lossy compressions remains a critical challenge, as psychoacoustic models irreversibly discard time-frequency information. Existing solutions primarily rely on intricate Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), which often struggle with training instability and the trade-off between perceptual quality and signal fidelity. In this paper, we propose MuDC-FM, a novel framework that reformulates music codec artifact removal as a Fourier-domain residual flow matching problem. Conditioned on the log-magnitude and phase of the compressed audio, our model predicts a complex-valued residual velocity field along a linear trajectory from the codec output to the clean target. This approach facilitates high-quality, non-autoregressive reconstruction via the Inverse Short-Time Fourier Transform (ISTFT) with few-step sampling. Experimental results demonstrate that MuDC-FM significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, achieving a 60% ∼ 110% improvement in objective metrics while accelerating inference speed by 2× ∼ 4×.

Performance Comparison

MuDC-FM demonstrates consistent and comprehensive superiority over existing compressed audio restoration methods across all tested scenarios. Regardless of the compression bitrate (128kbps, 96kbps, or 64kbps), it consistently outperforms Apollo[1] and SR-GAN[2] in both perceptual quality (MOS) and objective fidelity metrics (SDR, SI-SNR, PESQ). This uniform dominance across diverse compression conditions underscores its robustness and adaptability to varying degrees of information loss.
